Electricity Demand Model¶

A demand model for the electricity consumption of individuel households is beeing estimated. The data consists of time series for electricity consumption (as the endogen variable) and electricity prices and the individual GDP (as the exogen variables). The observed time frame starts in 1978 and ends in 2008. The data is prepared and adjusted for comparability by the correspondent index.

consumption = pd.read_csv('~/energy/demand.csv', sep=';', index_col=0).T

gdp_price = price.join(gdp) #gdp 1970, price 1978

gdp_price['price_real_Norway'] = gdp_price['price_norway']/gdp_price['index_norway']*100

energy_consumption = gdp_price.join(consumption)

Model Selection¶

A linear model is now fitted to the data. Four different models are beeing compared. They differ in the amount of variables used.

Model 1¶

$$E_t = K p_t^\alpha Y_t^\beta,$$

where $E_t$ is the demanded electricity at time $t$, $p_t$ is the price at time $t$, and $Y_t$ is the GDP. $\alpha$ is the price elasticity and $\beta$ is the income elasticity.

Transformed into a linear model results in: $$log(E_t) = log(K) + \alpha*log(p_t) + \beta*log(Y_t)$$

Model 3¶

The third model is based on model 1 and contains the lagged consumption.

$$E_t = K p_t^\alpha Y_t^\beta E_{t-1}^{\lambda},$$

$$log(E_t) = log(K) + \alpha*log(p_t) + \beta*log(Y_t) + \lambda*log(E_{t-1})$$

The data has to be extended so that it includes the lagged consumption.

Comparison of all for models for Finland¶

The first main difference between all 4 models is that different variables seem to be significant. Probably caused by the choice of variables in the model.

Model 1¶

All coefficiants n model 1 are significant (according to and significance level of 5%). The corrected degree of certainty is smaller than for all other models. Regarding the estimated coefficients one can see that the price elsticity represented by $\alpha$ is negativ. An increasing price would therefore result in and decreasing consumption. This is the expected behaviour. The estimated income elasticity $\beta$ is positiv. An increasing income would result in an increasing electrcity consumption. This also seems to confirm the expected behaviour.

Model 2¶

In model 2 the estimated income elasticity seems to be not significant, although very close. It seems tht more data would be needed to be sure. But since it is negative which contradicts to the expected behaviour this model can be neglected. Although all other coefficients are significant and the estimated coreected degree of certainty is quite high with $98%$ model 2 will be discarded.

Model 3¶

As soon as there is a lag added to the model all other coefficients expect the lag are not significant. Although the estimated signs relate to the expected behaviour of the elsticities. The demand of 'today' seems to be very strongly correlated with the demand of 'yesterday'. This strong (linear) relationshp can be seen in the plot below.

Final Model Selection for Finland and Norway¶

Model 3 and 4 had the best results for Norway. The differences between the models seem to be negligible, therefor model 3 will be the selected model since it has fewer variables.
